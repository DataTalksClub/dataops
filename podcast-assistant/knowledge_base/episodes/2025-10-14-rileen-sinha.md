Where does this story begin for you? Ph.D, alternative splicing, etc

Ph.D work on prediction of alternative splicing

Which events, successes, or stumbles taught you the most?

Features often matter more than model choice, e.g. BN & SVM were essentially tied (but a true expert in a given model can probably squeeze a lot more out of it )

With a sufficiently accurate model, you can even question the data (FPs turned out to be real AS events with updated evidence)

With a sufficiently strong model, FNs might point to something missed entirely by your model, and potentially a new mechanism - reanalyze just the FNs & see if something “pops up”. We found conserved secondary structure which occluded the splice site, but were pipped/scooped by someone else to the paper (they became a future collaborator and friend)

Is there a theme that keeps recurring in your work or study?

Validate, validate, validate!

Know the application domain

Make sure you’re indeed comparing apples to apples

Was there a detour that almost changed your direction?

That might be happening right now - I’m in between jobs, and might end up pivoting into AI/ML without Biology if I have to, though ideally I would combine them, e.g. in Early Cancer Detection.

What led you to transition from bioinformatics research in cancer genomics to applying machine learning and AI?

That hasn’t really happened, in the sense that my Ph.D research already applied machine learning, and I moved into cancer genomics at the post-doc stage. Since then, I’ve applied ML depending on the project requirements, and have taught myself from various resources from time to time. In the last 2 years, I’ve done more courses (esp. from Coursera and DataTalks Club), and competed on Kaggle.

Comparing cell lines and tumors, TCGA and other projects

When working on these projects, how do you balance biological complexity with computational constraints?

This usually varies, and detailed discussions with collaborators play a crucial part in determining this. One often has to pick up new biological knowledge to do proper justice to a project. There are of course also project where you’re only expected to perform some standard analyses to help answer a given set of questions. It’s also fairly common to start out with standard methods and then having to get deep into the weeds depending on the early findings.

How has participating in Kaggle competitions and hands-on ML courses shaped your approach to problem-solving and collaboration in computational biology?

Participating in Kaggle competitions and Hands-on ML has sharpened my appreciation of the importance of reproducibility in particular. For a long time, many of us in academia used to be content with proof-of-application code and moving to the next project and publication. More recently, there has been a growing awareness that reproducibility is a core requirement of good science, and ML Zoomcamp, LLM Zoomcamp and ML Engineering Zoomcamp helped me build good habits related to various aspects of reproducibility including virtual environments, flask, docker, cloud implementations, etc.

Multi-omics and AI-driven research are growing rapidly; what opportunities or insights are now possible that weren’t before?

The fascinating thing is that there are things that are possible because of advances in technology that generates large-scale data, and there are things that are possible because of advances in ML/AI, and these can feed off each other.

From your perspective, what are the main challenges in managing and analyzing large-scale biological datasets today?

I guess there are challenges on multiple fronts - as data grows, there are challenges with access and storage, efficient retrieval, etc. Large-scale data can also require specialized tools in coding - for example, once file sizes run into gigabytes, conventional file opening tools might struggle. As an example, conventional R code might struggle with reading huge input files, but specialized tools like the data.table package or the tidyverse package do a much better job. Those responsible for managing large-scale data are expected to be familiar with cluster computing, cloud computing and so on.

How do you see the role of computational biologists and AI/ML practitioners evolving in cancer research over the next few years

This is very complex, because AI itself could be responsible for a loss of jobs in cancer research, just like in so many areas. It’s easy to say that “you won’t be replaced by AI, but you will be replaced by another Computational Biologist using AI”, but organizations are definitely looking to replace people with AI, so there’s a real risk of a certain percentage of people losing jobs across industries and research areas, including cancer research, and even AI/ML themselves. What do you think?

Personal Insights & Advice

Is there a beginner group that keeps asking you about this?

The most frequent questions I get from beginners nowadays are about how to do better on Kaggle, owing to my having done well in the Tabular Playground Series on Kaggle, which are monthly competitions that typically attract 2000-4000 participants. I’ve secured 11 top 10 finishes in the last 1.5 years, including two 1st place finishes. So I tend to get a lot of questions from beginners about doing well on Kaggle in general, and tabular competitions in particular.

If listeners remember one idea next week, what should it be?

Validate, validate, validate!

Always know your domain, and be ready to learn more, and consult experts/collaborators as needed.

Are there any books or other resources that you can recommend to the listeners?

There is, of course, Alexey’s book for a great hands-on introduction which naturally pairs very well with the ML-Zoomcamp course. Three great books combining some theory with hands-on work in both ML and DL are Hands-On Machine Learning with Scikit-Learn, Keras, and TensorFlow by Aurélien Géron, Machine Learning with PyTorch and Scikit-Learn by Sebastian Raschka et al., and An Introduction to Statistical Learning by James, Witten, Hastie & Tibshirani; while those desiring more theory can look up books by Kevin Murphy, The Elements of Statistical Learning by Trevor Hastie, Robert Tibshirani and Jerome Friedman, or Shai Shalev-Shwartz & Shai Ben-David. Raschka also has a great YouTube channel, and his courses are great, as are those by Data Talks Club, and by Prof. Andrew Ng of Stanford and DeepLearning.AI.

What skills or approaches have been most valuable in your transition from bioinformatics to AI-driven research?

Not really applicable, since I’ve used ML off and on since the beginning, and am not doing AI-driven research in cancer genomics right now.

For students or early-career researchers interested in computational biology and ML, what would be a practical first step to get started?

This would depend strongly on their background, e.g. for people with a computational background, it would be very important to learn some relevant biology, and for biologists, to learn some programming and data science - then both can learn Computational Biology. This was in fact the approach followed in a postgraduate course I did in Cologne, Germany. In some ways, ML Zoomcamp reminded me of that course, as we learned new things every week, and were tested on them before moving on to the next topic.

How do you decide which research ideas or tools to pursue as public projects versus specialized internal analyses?

This would really depend on the organization, and project leadership. As a very rough guide, one might expect more of a public approach in academia and internal analyses in industry, but there are always exceptions.
