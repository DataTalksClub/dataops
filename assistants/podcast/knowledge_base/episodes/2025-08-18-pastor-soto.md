Final text based on Partor’s answers:

My path into AI began pragmatically: I needed income while studying medicine, and the side work I found ended up shaping my career. The real inflection point was ML Zoomcamp. By putting my work out in public during the course, I started getting interviews and offers without relying on freelancer platforms. That experience taught me two durable lessons: first, consistent connection with people compounds—many of my best opportunities trace back to conversations I started then; second, courses can be direct pipelines to jobs when you treat them as a place to build both skill and network.

A theme I see repeatedly—both in my story and in people I meet—is time-constrained learners trying to make room for ML. Many believe they need another degree to be considered; in practice, focused projects, visible output, and community engagement move the needle faster. My own milestones reflect that bias toward doing: I spent almost three years building a stock-market pipeline, which demanded serious engineering discipline and pushed me beyond toy examples. I shared what I learned on social media, which kept me accountable and surfaced collaborations. And I kept investing in learning; it’s been the highest-ROI decision of my career.

Today I work as an AI engineer. The work is less glamorous than it looks from the outside: most days are spent moving data, wiring APIs, and feeding LLMs—the plumbing that makes models useful. That continuity links my present to my start: solve real problems, show the work, iterate in public.

If there’s one takeaway, it’s this: treat learning as an intentional priority. Block time, ship small projects, and share them. If you have 15 minutes this week, pick a tiny task—run a single ML Zoomcamp exercise, post a short summary of what you learned, or wire one API call into a notebook. Small, finished steps compound.

### Event Description

Title: From Medicine to Machine Learning: How Public Learning Turned into a Career

How do you learn machine learning while studying medicine and turn it into a full-time career?

In this episode, we’re joined by Pastor Soto, a Machine Learning Engineer who learned ML through focused online learning and consistent public work. While still in medical school, Pastor took on side projects that led him into machine learning, and the ML Zoomcamp course (https://datatalks.club/blog/machine-learning-zoomcamp.html) served as a turning point in his career.

By sharing his work online, Pastor opened the door to job interviews, including at Meta and DeepLearning.AI.

We plan to cover:

How Pastor learned ML while studying medicine

How ML Zoomcamp helped Pastor to get interviews at companies like Meta and DeepLearning AI

Lessons for time-constrained learners: small wins, public updates, and ROI

How to position a non-traditional background (like an MD) in AI interviews

About the speaker:

Pastor Soto is a Machine Learning Engineer and mentor with a practical bias toward shipping. While studying medicine at the university, he took on side work that pulled him into machine learning. The inflection point was ML Zoomcamp: by publishing exercises and projects publicly, he attracted interviews and job offers.

Pastor’s work centers on production ML: moving data, wiring APIs, and feeding LLMs so applications run. He mentors with DeepLearning.AI, leads sections for Stanford’s Code in Place, and writes about focused learning and small, finished steps that compound.
