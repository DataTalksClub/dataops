### Detailed Questions

Physical AI as the next step of evolution. AI embodiment in the physical world, what to build in (motor controls etc) and what to learn (from the environment), “a theory of a thousand brains book”: old brain (survival, genes) and new brain (neo cortex - reasoning, discovery), designing intelligent machines by letting go of old brain (genes, biology) and building in intelligence (discovery, exploration)

Vision for life. We are alive in the unvierse, we have agency, we move around in physical space, we need to preserve that, i.e. help all of life to survive (biological life and artificial / created by humans / intelligent machines life)

Evolution of Machine Learning (ML) over the past 10-20 years. I was always fascinated with algorithms, that’s what got me into ML. ML 10 years ago was way more mathematical, if you wanted to innovate and design a new algorithm for example, you needed to be really good in math and coding. I used to do a lot more from scratch implementations (hence my book focusing on algorithmic ML from scratch) but I find now it’s either foundation model training or fine-tuning and synthetic data generation for training. It’s much less from scratch.

Impact of copilot on society and jobs. I see copilot as an assistant, knowledge on demand, it helps me fix bugs and saves time doing research myself on-line. However, you have to be careful because it’s not a replacement for thinking. There’s a temptation to just ask chat-gpt a question you have in mind first and read the information (collective knowledge) without taking the time to brainstorm. There was a study saying that chat-gpt users use less of their brain power. That’s not good. Similarly, AI agents can automate tasks traditionally performed by humans. For example, I love tutoring and I’m trying to understand if tutoring will still exist in the chat-gpt era.

Leaving a legacy. Life is short and focusing your energy on helping improve people's lives and helping all of life to survive is a worthy pursuit. Leaving a legacy, something that lasts with time, is important to me. I’ve been thinking about two categories and I’m curious to hear about more: physical and digital artifacts. Physical (when you build something in the physical world) and digital (when you create or record something in the digital world)

Setting goals. Early on I got into self-help literature and it helped me gain confidence in highly competitive environments (in college) and it introduced me to goal setting. I really like that philosophy of goal setting, seeing yourself as an agent and a designer of your life. Defining a vision for what you want, defining the goals that will help you achieve that vision, asking effective yourself effective questions (i’m working on a new illustration book right now that describes that in more detail - like guide principles for life without tons of pages, brief, high signal, things that work). Stay tuned.

Resources for learning and building with LLMs. I recommend LLM Engineer’s Handbook and Building LLM from scratch books to learn the foundations. Also, OpenAI documentation is really helpful if you want to for example build your own agent from scratch (not using any of the frameworks) you can build a client in python that supports multi-turn conversations with tool calling and that would be a good exercise. Also, TRL library by HuggingFace is really helpful for both fine-tuning LLMs and learning by reading documentation. I recommend getting experience with LoRA parameter efficient fine-tuning as well as techniques for synthetic data generation (for fine-tuning). Evaluating quality of LLM generated output is another important area, e.g. getting experience with LLM-as-a-judge and writing judge prompts.

Algorithms for LLM alignment. Alignment means steering LLM response to match human preferences / requirements. Starting with SFT (supervised fine-tuning) where we need to provide sample outputs, to GRPO (group-relative policy optimization used to train DeepSeek-R1) where we sample multiple responses and fine-tune the model to mimic better responses, DPO (direct preference optimization) that takes in chosen and rejected pairs (good and bad examples), RLHF (reinforcement-learning from human feedback) collects ranking preferences from human annotators and uses PPO (proximal policy optimization) for fine-tuning the LLM. All of these algorithms are implemented in HuggingFace TRL library (https://huggingface.co/docs/trl/). Another great reference is:

RLHF Book by Nathan Lambert (https://rlhfbook.com/)

### Event Description

Opening Snapshot

I’ve always been fascinated with algorithms, at first computer science algorithms and later machine learning algorithms. The human brain is the most complex biological computer that differentiates us from other species and creating artificial brain / intelligence excites me.

Key Moments & Lessons

Do what you are passionate about. While working on my master’s in wireless architecture I discovered that the thing I enjoy doing the most are algorithms and I wanted to pursue that in greater depth, which led me to a PhD program in machine learning. Similarly in choosing which teams to work with in the industry, I prioritize interesting work and interesting people.

Another important theme is adaptation in work and personal life. AI is a fast changing field and you are missing out if you are not working on the latest technology.

Students deciding on their majors, graduate students deciding on the company to join, researchers deciding where to concentrate their efforts

Title: From Algorithms to Agents: Lessons from Building Copilot

Subtitle: What Generative AI Has Changed and Which Fundamentals Still Matter - Vadim Smolyakov

Vadim’s path spans from algorithmic ML and MIT research to building Copilot at Microsoft, providing him with a front-row view of how practice has shifted from hand-built algorithms to agentic systems.

In this conversation, he reflects on what genuinely changed with generative AI and what fundamentals still matter.

We’ll discuss assistants as complements to human thinking, why embodiment (“physical AI”) may be the next step, and how goal-setting and sharing knowledge shape work that lasts.

We plan to cover:

What Copilot taught about scope, trade-offs, and evaluation

Agents as assistants: avoiding over-reliance while gaining real leverage

Fine-tuning, synthetic data, and how the craft of ML has evolved

Physical AI: what to build in vs. what to learn from the environment

Purpose, habits, and leaving durable digital/physical artifacts
