### Event Description

Title: From Theme Parks to Tesla: Building Data Products That Work

Subtitle: A conversation with Abouzar Abbaspour on ML engineering, data pipelines, and real-world impact

What does it take to turn raw data into products that millions of people love to use? Abouzar Abbaspour will discuss this based on his experience working with startups, amusement parks, e-commerce, and now Tesla.

At Efteling theme park, he built queue-time forecasting and recommendation systems for visitors. At bol.com, he helped deploy a recommendation engine to over 6 million users. And today at Tesla, he works on predictive maintenance and integrates LLM agents into production systems.

In this conversation, Abouzar shares stories from the front lines of data and ML engineering: what succeeds, what fails, and what matters when you move from experiments to production.

We plan to cover:

Forecasting queues and building recommendation systems at a theme park

Deploying large-scale recommender systems at bol.com

The leap from data engineering into ML engineering

Predictive maintenance and LLM agents at Tesla

Why productionizing ML is about much more than the model

Which trends in data and ML are hype, and which are here to stay

### Speech

Today on the podcast, we’re joined by Abouzar Abbaspour.

He’s worked on data and ML projects in very different places — from amusement parks to e-commerce and now Tesla. We’ll talk about how to turn data into real products, what it means to productionize ML, and what the future of data and ML engineering might look like.

Abouzar, welcome to the podcast!

Abouzar, your career spans startups, amusement parks, e-commerce, and now Tesla. Can you walk us through your journey?

Questions:

1. Personal reflections

You co-founded a startup, worked in academia, and are now in big tech. Which of these gave you the biggest learning curve?

You grew up in Iran and then built your career in Europe. How has your background shaped the way you solve problems?

2. Stories from the amusement park (Efteling)

At Efteling, you built models to predict queue times and recommend next best actions. How did you turn raw visitor data into something useful?

What did you learn about making ML models work well for real people in such a physical, customer-facing place?

3. From theme parks to 6M users at bol.com

At bol.com, you worked on a recommendation engine that later served 6 million users. How did you test and validate the model before going live at that scale?

Can you share a story where business or product limits shaped the final solution more than the technical side?

4. Bridging data engineering and ML engineering

You started as a senior data engineer and later moved into ML engineering. Which skills carried over, and what was the hardest part to learn in the new role?

In your view, what do data engineers often misunderstand about ML, and what do ML engineers often misunderstand about data engineering?

5. Tesla and production ML at scale

At Tesla, you split your time between data engineering and ML. Can you tell us about a project where predictive maintenance models or LLM agents directly improved workflows?

What does deployment and monitoring look like in such a high-stakes environment? How do you balance moving fast with keeping things safe and reliable?

6. Productionizing

You’ve said it’s important to think about the end user, not just the model. What are the common mistakes teams make when moving from a Jupyter notebook to a real product?

Can you share an example where improving the pipeline — things like Airflow, CI/CD, or Kubernetes — made a big difference for the ML product?

7. Problem-solving mindset

You describe yourself as a problem solver. Can you share a story where limits or constraints forced you into a creative but effective solution?

8. Industry evolution

You’ve seen the industry shift from Hadoop to GCP pipelines to today’s LLM agents. Which of these changes are true game-changers, and which are more hype?

How do you see the roles of data engineer and ML engineer changing in the next few years? Do you think they will merge, or become even more separate?

9. Closing

If you could give one piece of advice to someone who wants to build impactful ML or data products today, what would it be?

Where can listeners follow your work and see the visualizations you’ve prepared?

Are there any books or other resources you would recommend to the listeners?

Core Themes to Explore

1. Data + ML at Tesla (current role)

Predictive maintenance with ML: how do you design and deploy models that directly impact physical systems (cars, factories)?

Integrating LLM RAGs and agents into existing apps: what works, what’s hype, and how do you make them useful in production?

Building custom Airflow operators and CI/CD pipelines: lessons learned in making data/ML workflows actually reliable at scale.

Kubernetes + ML systems: how to balance scalability, cost, and team productivity.

2. Transition from Data Engineering to ML Engineering

How do data engineering foundations prepare someone for ML system design?

Advice for people who want to make the same transition.

3. Real-world Recommendation Systems

At bol.com: recommendation engine for 6M+ users. What did it take to validate and deploy it?

At Efteling: queue forecasting + “next best action” recommendations in a theme park setting. What unique data challenges appear outside the “classic tech company” environment?

How do you balance business goals, user experience, and technical constraints in recommender systems?

4. From Startup Founder → Engineer in Big Tech

Netgat: building a data startup monitoring telecom networks, lessons in handling raw sensor data at scale.

5. Evolution of the Data & ML Landscape

He’s worked across: telecom, theme parks, e-commerce, automotive. What are the recurring data problems across industries, and what’s unique in each?

Trends he’s seen: Hadoop → GCP pipelines → LLM agents. What’s hype vs. real progress?

Where he sees the data/ML engineer role going in the next 5–10 years.

6. Personal Perspective

Coming from Iran → TU Eindhoven → Europe tech → Tesla: reflections on navigating global tech career paths.
