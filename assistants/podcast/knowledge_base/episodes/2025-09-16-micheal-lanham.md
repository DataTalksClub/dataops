### Speech

Today on the podcast, we’re joined by Micheal Lanham.

Micheal’s career spans two decades, from building game AI with neural networks and evolutionary algorithms, to writing more than ten books on AR/VR, reinforcement learning, and AI agents. Today, he focuses on advanced deep learning, evolutionary methods, and building intelligent systems that go beyond LLMs.

In this conversation, we’ll talk about what makes AI agents different from simple chatbots, how games and simulations continue to shape AI research, and what has really changed in practice as the field moved from early neural nets to today’s generative AI.

Micheal, welcome to the podcast!

Micheal, you’ve worked in games, enterprise software, geoscience, and now AI agents. Can you walk us through your journey, and what connects all these fields?

Questions:

You started experimenting with neural networks and evolutionary algorithms in the early 2000s. How did that experience shape how you look at AI today?

You’ve built AI for games and also written books on game development. What can the AI industry learn from the way games use data and intelligence?

Do you think reinforcement learning and simulation-driven AI will become more important again in the era of LLMs?

In your book Evolutionary Deep Learning, you argue for combining genetic algorithms with neural networks. Where do you see the biggest promise of this approach?

Your latest book is AI Agents in Action. How do you define an “AI agent,” and how is it different from just using an LLM with prompts?

What kinds of real-world problems do you think agents are best suited for today — and which ones are still hype?

You’ve also written about AR, VR, and immersive environments. How do you see AI agents and XR coming together?

You’ve worked in very different industries, from oil and gas to fintech to software. What lessons about taking ML to production apply everywhere?

You’ve published more than 10 technical books. How does writing help you make sense of such a fast-moving field?

When teaching others, what fundamentals do you find people struggle with most — and which ones remain timeless despite all the hype?

You’ve seen the field shift from early neural nets to today’s GPT agents. What has really changed in practice, and what hasn’t?

Looking forward, where do you think data and AI teams should focus in the next 5 years?

Are there any books or other resources that you can recommend to the listeners?
