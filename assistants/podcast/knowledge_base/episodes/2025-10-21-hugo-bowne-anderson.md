# Hugo Bowne-Anderson - How to build and evaluate AI systems in the age of LLMs

## Subtitle

A practical guide for data scientists and engineers - Hugo Bowne-Anderson

## Bio

Hugo Bowne-Anderson is an independent data and AI consultant with extensive experience in the tech industry. He has advised and taught teams building AI-powered systems, including engineers from Netflix, Meta, and the U.S. Air Force. He is the host of Vanishing Gradients (https://vanishinggradients.fireside.fm/) and High Signal (https://high-signal.delphina.ai/), podcasts exploring developments in data science and AI. Previously, Hugo served as Head of Developer Relations at Outerbounds and held roles at Coiled and DataCamp, where his work in data science education reached over 6 million learners. He has taught at Yale University, Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory, and conferences like SciPy and PyCon, and is a passionate advocate for democratizing data skills and open-source tools. He also regularly teaches courses on Building LLM Applications for Data Scientists and Software Engineers (https://maven.com/s/course/d56067f338).

Questions:

When (and When Not) to Build AI Agents

When you look at real-world use cases, how do you decide if an AI agent is the right approach versus a simpler tool or workflow?

Answer the following questions:

Does it need to do something or is returning text enough?

Does the AI system need to trigger it’s own actions or can we define them in workflows?

Stepping back, let’s see “agents or llm” as a spectrum, rather than a binary:

First you have LLM; then memory, retrieval, tools; then workflows

What patterns or principles have you noticed that help teams avoid over-engineering with agents?

Write a PRD

Start small

Evaluate

iterate

How should teams evaluate whether an agent-based system is working as intended before scaling it?

Evaluate macro business goals as much as possible and tie them to more fine-grained metrics

AI for Coding vs. Building Software with LLMs

How do you distinguish between using AI as a coding assistant and actually building software that’s powered by LLMs?

You can use AI coding assistants for lots of SW that doesn’t involve LLMs at all, e.g. front end, data analysis, EDA, classic ML

I’d encourage everyone to. let’s briefly look at the wild rapid evolution of AI-assisted coding here:

Level 1: C&P code between ChatGPT & IDE

Level 2: Code completion in IDE

Level 3: agent in IDE/terminal (degrees of how much it breaks down multi-step tasks)

Level 4: AI embedded in other tools: slack, discord, email (whatsapp?)

Level 5: AI more proactive (e.g. code review in CI, gh actions)

Level 6: background agents

Level 7: (haven’t really seen yet) proactive AI – notices stuff in prod, comes to me on a monday and says hey check this out!

What kinds of design or engineering challenges appear once LLMs become core components of the software itself?

Nondeterminism of input and output

Complexity of the system

Think multi-agent crews

Need a good human-in-the-loop

Even looking at your data can be tough

How do you see the role of developers and data scientists evolving as these systems mature?

Increasingly important

Think about the skillsets

We need people who can think and talk data

We also need people who can build and maintain enterprise systems and software

The AI Software Development Lifecycle

You’ve written about “escaping PoC purgatory.” What typically keeps AI projects stuck in that phase, and how can teams break through it?

Let’s talk through this figure (https://www.oreilly.com/radar/wp-content/uploads/sites/3/2025/04/LLM-SDLC_Fig1_edit3-1.png)

Great ideas that don’t solve problems: eg edTech company creating an all-purpose AI tutor instead of noticing they could solve 20% of customer support tickets with RAG-BOT that helped learners find the content they were looking

Re nondeterminism pain, the iteration / eval loop can really help (but in it, you can run the same prompts through the system many times! And see the variance of results; then as you iterate, work to reduce the variance)

Also use tests (eg pytest) for the output; regenerate in prod if it doesn’t satisfy certain criteria

Also note that nondeterminism can be a good thing: e.g. research analysts: if i send it out 10 times, i’d want 10 different results that I (or an LLM) can synthesize

What does evaluation-driven development look like in day-to-day practice?

Build MVP (including logging!)

Generate user personas, scenarios, and synthetic data

Run them through your MVP

Hand label 20, 50, 100 traces as pass/fail with reason

Cluster into failure modes

You can then see what needs fixing!

This may involve prompt eng, changing chunking strategies, embeddings; reranking, adding/removing tool calls; structured outputs; temp; top-p; how you handle memory for multi-turn convos ← but a lot of the time

How can teams balance rapid experimentation with the need for robust monitoring and testing in production AI systems?

Tooling and process

What Happens to Data Science in the Age of AI

With LLMs now handling parts of data exploration and modeling, how do you see the identity of data scientists changing?

Opens them up for more creative work

Opens them up to think about the business more

Allows them to design systems instead of debugging pandas code

Which skills or ways of thinking will remain critical for data scientists as AI systems evolve?

Failure analysis

How can organizations make sure human judgment still guides interpretation and decision-making in AI workflows?

Having people who are close to the business and the data
