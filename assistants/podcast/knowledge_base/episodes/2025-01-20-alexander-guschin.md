Possible Topics:

About the topics. I think we can cover three parts of my career in the interview:

- First - talk about Kaggle. I'm Kaggle Competitions Grandmaster (5th place in the world in 2017 https://www.kaggle.com/aguschin). This was my ticket to DS and ML field. At that time, in Russia there were no practical ML-oriented courses or programs in university, which is still a big problem for some countries. Kaggle was a place I was able to learn from nothing and I believe it's still a good place for many people to start their career when there are no better alternatives at hand.

- Second - talk about my industrial DS/ML career that started after Kaggle. Since my career started back then many things about starting a career aren't very relevant now (may be interesting from a "historical" viewpoint, but it's up to you to decide if that is worth it), but what's interesting is how it advanced. I started as a Data Analyst (at Avito), then realized I don't have neither sufficient Python/Programming skills nor deep ML/statistical knowledge and it's hurting the projects I'm working on. I tried to fix missing ML/statistical knowledge in the next place of work (Yandex) where I was DS. Yandex was great but it's a big company so my scope of work was pretty narrow (fit ML model and make it useful to business, but don't care about production things). I saw that as a problem and decided to develop myself in that direction. To learn more about ML production and become an ML Engineer, I moved to a small startup. In that startup, we tried to build a niche closed-source product around autoML for Industrial enterprises. There I realized another thing: you can be a brilliant ML Engineer, know Python and how to deploy everything, but eventually it's really cool to work on products that help people do their stuff. So I started to work at iterative.ai (http://iterative.ai/) (you may know them for DVC dvc.org (http://dvc.org/)) to build an MLflow models alternative with alternative pros and cons. That was pretty exciting until the startup ran out of money, making me realize some hard things about working at startups. That was a pretty long explanation of my career path, but eventually I tried different hats and became what some call Fullstack ML Engineer (in contrast with some folks who are excellent in a more narrow scope, which is also pretty awesome). This has some pros and cons, but is an interesting profile to have as I find it.

- Third - talk about teaching ML and my current job. I started teaching 10 years ago, as soon as I started winning ML competitions. Back in 2017 we created a class about how to win ML competitions for Coursera, and around 100k people watched it. Then I was teaching offline in Moscow (both competitive ML and Production ML), then I recorded an online class about MLOps. But it was always a hobby for me - although I liked it a lot. Until last year (2024) I got an interesting offer from a new university in Moscow (https://centraluniversity.ru (https://centraluniversity.ru/)) whose goal is to give a good industrial education on a scale. For some years I wanted to try to do some good things in education, but it was scary to abandon an industrial ML career (who knows how hard it will be to get back to industry after few years of full-time in education) + I didn't heard about any really fast-growing initiative in education that was worth a switch in my case (I mean there should be big goals & fast pace & enough money involved & a good spot for me specifically, since there are really a lot of good ML folks out there who can make a good things in education, although I think not a lot will agree to pause their industrial career for that). When I got that offer, I had just received a UK Global Talent visa and was living in the UK for a couple of months already and going through some interviews with tech startups. It was a hard call, but I decided to try this opportunity with that university in Moscow and I hadn't ever regretted it. I do a lot of interesting stuff - launch classes on Production ML and ML System Design, develop LLM-based graders that can grade students' text assignments (such as an ML System Design Doc) and train Russian team International Olympiad in AI (last year we took the "absolute" 1st place and gold medals in this olympiad - https://ioai-official.org/results/. Our team name was "Letovo" and not "Russia" due to the reasons I'm sure you are aware of).
