### Event Description

Title: Mindful Data Strategy: From Pipelines to Business Impact

Subtitle: Using Zen Principles to Bridge Technical Excellence and Strategic Outcomes - Lior Barak

Despite 97% of organizations investing in data, only 24% of executives feel truly data-driven. Too often, data teams build impressive pipelines and dashboards that never connect back to business results.

In this podcast episode, Lior Barak, Data Zen Master and author of Data Is Like Hummus, shares how ancient Zen principles, paired with modern data strategy frameworks, can help teams close the gap between technical effort and business impact.

What we plan to cover:

The Data Strategy Triangle: How to balance maintenance, innovation, and rollout, and align your work with long-term organizational goals.

Executive KPIs that Matter: The two metrics every data team should track to tie technical output to revenue growth, cost reduction, or retention.

Wabi-Sabi for Data Products: How embracing imperfection and transparency builds trust faster than chasing unattainable “perfect” data.

The Data Team Stress Index: A tool to prioritize work, anticipate burnout, and make strategic decisions about tooling and staffing as your team grows.

About the speaker:

Lior Barak is an independent data strategy consultant and former hands-on practitioner. He’s helped organizations in tech, finance, and healthcare evolve from reactive firefighting to sustainable, high-impact data practices.

He’s also the creator of:

The Data Ecosystem Vision Board: a visual planning tool for aligning data efforts across teams

The Data Product Lifecycle Manager: a framework for tracking ROI across a data product’s entire lifespan

Lior also writes Cooking Data, a newsletter that pairs mindful data strategy with nourishing, practical advice. As he says: “Data is like hummus: it should be simple and nourishing.”

### Speech

This week, we're focusing on mindful data strategy and how data teams can shift from building pipelines to delivering real business outcomes. We'll look at how principles from Zen philosophy can help teams work more intentionally and effectively.

We have a special guest today, Lior Barak. Lior is a data strategy consultant, former practitioner, and author of Data Is Like a Plate of Hummus. He developed the Data Ecosystem Vision Board and the Data Product Lifecycle Manager - frameworks designed to help teams move from reactive tasks to more strategic, long-term thinking.

Welcome, Lior!

Before we begin, Lior, could you briefly describe your career journey and what led you to create these frameworks? (not too long please =))

Questions:

You describe yourself as a “Data Zen Master.” When did you realize that Zen principles could apply to data strategy?

Can you explain the Data Ecosystem Vision Board? What elements does it include, and how does it support better decision-making?

You use the term “Strategy Triangle” to describe maintenance, innovation, and rollout. What does this framework represent?

Should teams aim to balance those three areas? How can they recognize imbalance, and what’s a good first step to address it?

You’ve brought the Wabi-Sabi concept into data strategy — the idea of finding value in imperfection. How do you apply this in real data work?

Many stakeholders ask for perfect data. How do you explain that transparency and iteration often build more trust than perfectionism?

You've said there are two KPIs that matter most to executives. What are they, and why focus on just two?

What is the Data Team Stress Index? How is it used, and what value does it offer to growing teams?

You’ve mentioned that the industry often overcomplicates problems. Where do you see the most inefficiency, and what’s one way to simplify?

Given that data is never perfect, how do you decide when a model or dashboard is “good enough” to release?

You’ve said AI won’t replace data teams, but it will change how we work. What shifts should teams start making now?

For those listening today, what’s one small “data impact check” they can run in 15 minutes to assess their team’s focus?

To close, a few quick personal questions:

Your favorite hummus topping?

A Zen idea that influences how you lead?

One non-technical book you recommend for data leaders?
