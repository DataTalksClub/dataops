### Speech

Today on the podcast, we’re joined by Aishwarya Jadhav — Machine Learning Engineer at Waymo, formerly part of Tesla’s Autopilot AI team, and a Carnegie Mellon University alum.

Aishwarya has worked across some of the toughest applied AI problems: financial recommender systems at Morgan Stanley, multimodal research at CMU, perception and video understanding at Tesla, and now gesture and pedestrian semantics at Waymo. She’s also contributed to AI for social good, including a malaria mapping project in Africa that achieved real-world impact at scale.

Aishwarya, welcome to the podcast!

What connects these very different domains for you?

Questions:

What was the most challenging adjustment when moving from financial recommendation systems at Morgan Stanley to perception models at Tesla and Waymo?

At Tesla you worked on video understanding and multimodal LLMs, and at Waymo you lead projects on gesture and pedestrian semantics. What practical problems do teams face when turning research models into production systems for self-driving?

When you evaluate models for pedestrian or gesture recognition, what metrics or validation methods matter most to decide if they are safe enough to deploy?

In your CMU research and later roles, you worked on combining vision, text, and trajectory data. Where have you seen multimodality clearly improve results, and where did it add complexity without much benefit?

What’s an example of a trade-off you’ve had to make between model accuracy and real-time performance in multimodal or perception systems?

Your malaria vector mapping project reduced mosquito populations significantly at very low cost. What design choices made the model both accurate and lightweight enough for low-resource settings?

Looking back, what lessons from that project have stayed with you when working on high-budget, high-compute projects in big tech?

Organizing AI related projects

Researchy, need multiple streams or groups working on different experiments

Cannot rely completely on one method, need multiple parallel

Making trade-offs still relates to having accurate information about what are the accuracy / error expectations

That governs a whole lot of decision choices you make: from the kind of data you include to what metrics you choose to compare and pick models

Analyzing deeply to uncover issues and building based off of those insights rather than blindly just trying everything

In your experience, what are the main bottlenecks preventing broader deployment of self-driving systems today — is it perception accuracy, edge cases, or something else?

Technological problems: edge-cases, world is constantly evolving and people or other road entities can be very dynamic. Although the SDC is much safer than a human driver (given its faster reaction times), there are still a few open research problems in terms of communicating with the world around it

Wide-spread adoption - there is still the people aspect, do not trust it and are not fully accepting of it → ties into gov and policies.
